Overview

In this case study my team and I were tasked with redesigning the website for a nonprofit organization of our choosing. After a brief deliberation, we landed on the Make-A-Wish Foundation. One of our team members had first hand experience with the organization as a child when her brother’s wish to fly on the Air Force One was granted. Based on our assumption that parents or other family members were the primary ones referring children, our initial plan was to redesign the existing child referral pages, however our research showed that medical staff were the main ones referring the children directly to coordinators without the use of the website.

Research

Our initial research consisted of user interviews and a google survey. We targeted three separate users; the parents/guardians of wish recipients, volunteers, and Make-A-Wish staff. We organized our findings using an affinity diagram so that we could better understand the needs of the users. We found that most of the users' pain points involved issues with communication. In one particularly heart wrenching case, one of our interviewees spoke in depth about a period of six months that was devoid of any communication from their Wish Coordinator. He said that he felt like there wasn’t any progress being made toward completing his child's wish and he was afraid that his child might not be well enough to even enjoy his wish by the time it was finished. Meanwhile, there was a lot going on behind the scenes that just wasn’t being communicated. We were also surprised to find that there are no resources for parents on the website itself.

Problem Statement

Wish recipients/applicants often feel that communication with wish coordinators is subpar and need an easier way to receive updates about their children's wishes or qualification status.

Hypothesis

We believe wish recipients need a way to track the progress of their application and the wish granting process on the Make-A-Wish website.
